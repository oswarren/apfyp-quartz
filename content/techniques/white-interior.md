**Why is the inside white?** On a run of vessels in [[range-1848-1899|pieces 1848–1899]], a dark, gritty exterior tears open at the rim to reveal an interior lined bright white to cream — usually webbed with fine crazing, like dried river mud shrunk into plates. The contrast is the whole drama: [[1894]] opens a scalloped torn rim onto heavy white crazing, [[1892]] pairs its white lining with a rust-crumbled seam band, and [[1890]] wears the look inside while zebra bands wrap the outside.

The term names the bright lining and its crazing, not the material behind it. Partial cases stay in prose: [[1888]]'s glossy pale inner rim is described on its own page, untagged.
