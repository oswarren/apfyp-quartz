**Why does the surface look like dried mud?** Two reviewed pieces wear a pale crust that has crazed into a web of plates over a contrasting body — the look of lichen on rock, or a lakebed after the water leaves. On [[1872]] the crust flakes lichen-like over a dark vessel; on [[1983]] it sits as flat mud-crack plates over a rust-orange chawan. Same event, two grounds.

Whether the crust is slip, glaze, or something else that shrank harder than its body is never asserted — and per the catalog's condition discipline, the crazing is a surface look, never called flaking, failing, or a defect. The term waited as a one-member candidate from the batch-9 review until [[1983]] gave it a second member in the 2026-07-12 batch-11 ruling.
