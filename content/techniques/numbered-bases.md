**Why is there a number on the base?** Every piece in this project carries its sequence number on its base, written by hand. The number isn't decoration — it's the piece's identity and its price: piece 2,254 is listed at $22.54, because the price of any piece is its number times one cent. Turn the piece over and you're reading both its place in the 10,000-piece archive and its price tag.

## Two marking styles

The pieces documented so far show two ways the number gets onto the base:

- **Hand-painted** — the number brushed on in dark pigment ([[2250]], [[2251]], [[2252]], [[2253]])
- **Incised** — the number carved directly into the clay ([[2254]], [[2255]], [[2256]] and onward through [[2260]])

In the 2250–2260 range, the switch from painted to incised happens **between pieces 2253 and 2254** — in the middle of the June 16 photography session, not at a session boundary. Six pieces photographed the same day split across both styles. Whether either style is the rule for the wider 10,000-piece run isn't something these eleven pieces can establish; they only show that the convention was changing right here.

## Why it's worth looking at

A numbered base is the project's whole mechanism in miniature: the archive (this number exists once), the ledger (the number is the price), and the hand (no two numerals are written alike).
