**What is handbuilding?** Handbuilding is shaping clay directly with the hands — pinching, coiling, and pressing — rather than throwing it on a potter's wheel. It's one of the oldest ways to make pottery, and it leaves fingerprints, literally: uneven rims, walls that thicken and thin, forms that lean and settle.

## How to spot it

A wheel-thrown cup is symmetric; spin it and its silhouette doesn't change. A handbuilt cup is a record of hands. On the pieces in this series, look for:

- **Uneven, hand-formed rims** — the rim rises and dips instead of running level
- **Irregular silhouettes** — rounder here, flatter there; piece [[2256]] even shows a facet in its profile
- **Variation between pieces** — no two are the same shape, even within one batch photographed days apart

## Why it matters in this series

Every piece in this 10,000-piece project is made by hand, one at a time, and numbered in sequence. The irregularity isn't a defect to be sanded away — it's the evidence that piece 2,253 is one specific object, distinct from 2,252 before it and 2,254 after. The penny-per-piece pricing counts the pieces; the handbuilding is what makes each one worth counting.
