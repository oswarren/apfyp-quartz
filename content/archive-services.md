You made the work. You shouldn't also have to become an archivist, a database designer, a web developer, and a search specialist just so people can find it and understand it.

But that's roughly what a serious body of work ends up asking of you. Photographs accumulate across phones, cameras, and old computers. Titles drift, or never existed. A spreadsheet gets started and abandoned. Notes live in three places. None of this means anything went wrong. It's the normal residue of actually making things, year after year. It just means the work gets hard to see as a whole, for you and for anyone trying to find it.

This site is what the alternative looks like. [A Penny For Your Pottery](about) began as hundreds of listing photographs, partial store records, inconsistent titles, and a numbered series already thousands of pieces long. Turning that into the archive you're browsing (a page for every photographed piece, connected ranges and recurring details, careful language about what is and isn't known) is the same work I now offer to other artists.

## More examples of the method

The system isn't a template. It gets shaped around whatever is being archived. Two working examples:

**[A Penny For Your Pottery](./)** is this site: a growing archive of a numbered ceramic project. It connects individual pieces, their photographs, ranges of the series, recurring details, and confirmed records into one browsable whole, and it keeps growing as new pieces are photographed and reviewed.

**[Rock Archive](https://oswarren.github.io/rock-archive-quartz/)** is my growing rock collection, being built into a digital archive with the same method. Raw photographs enter a private archive, each specimen gets a structured record, and what can be observed is kept separate from what is only suspected, so nothing is presented as definitively identified unless it has been. Related materials, locations, themes, and research can be connected over time, and only reviewed information reaches the public site.

One is a numbered body of ceramic work. The other is a personal physical collection with observation, identification, and research attached. Same system, different shape, because the shape follows the material. This page is written for artists, but the process fits collectors and independent researchers with substantial visual collections just as well.

## What I do

You hand over what you already have, in whatever state it's in. Folders nested inside folders, a phone full of photos, a half-finished spreadsheet, notes in the margins of a sketchbook: none of it needs to be organized first. Organizing it is the job you're hiring me for.

From there I:

- go through the source files and group what belongs together
- match photographs to the works they show
- keep every confirmed fact exactly as you recorded it
- identify the gaps, and come back to you with the questions worth asking
- build a structured record for each work
- connect works into series, time periods, and shared ideas: the paths a visitor would actually want to follow
- build the private archive that holds all of it
- build the public website on top of it
- set up a review step so nothing uncertain is published as fact
- leave you a simple workflow for adding new work as you make it

Automation helps with the tedious parts: sorting files, drafting records, catching inconsistencies. But nothing is blindly generated. Everything that reaches the public site is reviewed and shaped, and anything the process can't verify is either asked about, worded as an observation, or left out. You can see that rule at work all over this site: pages describe what the photographs show and quote the store's records, and they don't state a detail as fact unless the person who made the work confirmed it. In your archive, that confirming voice is you.

## What you receive

### The private archive

The part only you see. This is the source of truth the website is built from:

- a canonical record for every work included
- the source photographs, organized and connected to their works
- your private notes and anything you don't want published
- the relationships between works, series, dates, and ideas, recorded in a form that will still make sense years from now
- a structure you (or anyone you work with later) can keep adding to

### The public website

The part everyone else sees, built from the archive and showing only what you've chosen to publish:

- a page for each work
- pages for series, periods, themes, materials, or processes, where your body of work actually calls for them
- search, plus several ways to browse, so different visitors find different ways in
- a design shaped around your work, not a generic inventory grid

A well-structured archive also gives search engines and AI systems something clear to read: which works exist, how they relate, and what is actually true about them. I don't promise rankings or traffic (nobody honestly can), but structure is the foundation those systems work from, and most artist websites don't have it.

## Who this is for

You don't need an enormous collection. The service fits best if some of this sounds familiar:

- several years of work behind you
- many photographs, incomplete records
- you work in series, or the same materials, techniques, places, or ideas keep returning
- you want an archive of the work, not only a portfolio of the highlights
- you've been putting off rebuilding your website because organizing everything first feels impossible

That last one matters. The organizing-first step is exactly the step I remove.

## How it works

1. You send a representative sample of what you have (some photographs, whatever records exist) and we have a short conversation about the work.
2. I build a small piece of the archive from that sample and go through the uncertain parts with you, so you can see how the process treats your work before you commit to more.
3. Once you approve, I complete the agreed archive, publish the site, and hand you a simple workflow for future additions.
